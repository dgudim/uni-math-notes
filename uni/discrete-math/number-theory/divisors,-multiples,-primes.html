Multiples, divisors

Definition
  • Integer a is a multiple of some integer b if we can write a=bm
  • Non-zero integer b divides integer a or b|a (there are several notions), if number a is it’s multiple.
  • Then number b is called a divisor of number a
Theorem

If a, b and c are integers, then

  • For all a: a|a
  • For all a, b and c: if a|b and b|c, then a|c
  • For all a, b and c: b|a and b|c if and only if b|(m∗a+n∗c) with any integer numbers m and n
Theorem
  • If a and b are natural numbers and a|b, then a≤b
  • If a and b are natural numbers, a|b and b|a, then a=b or a=−b

Division with remainder

Theorem

If a and b are natural numbers, then there is only one pair of natural numbers q and r, where 0≤r<b, such that a=bq+r

Theorem

If a=bq+c, then gcd(a;b)=gcd(b;c)

Example
  • 17=3*5+2; gcd(17;3)=1, gcd(3;2)=1
  • 33=6*5+3; gcd(33;6)=3, gcd(6;3)=3

Common divisor

Definition

Natural number d is common divisor of numbers a and b, if d|a and d|b

Greatest common divisor

Definition
  • Natural number d is called greatest common divisor of numbers a and b, if
    • It is a common divisor
    • If c|a and c|b, then c|d (c - any common divisor) (should be divisible by all other common divisors)
  • $ Notion gcd(a;b)
Example

Common divisors of 1540 and 294 are 1, 2, 7, 14
gcd(1540,294)=14; (2|14, 7|14)

Euclid's algorithm

If a and b are natural numbers, and

a=bq0+r0     0≤r0≤bb=r0q1+r1     0≤r1≤r0r0=r1q2+r2     0≤r2≤r1r1=r2q3+r3     0≤r3≤r2r2=r3q4+r4     0≤r4≤r3

  • Let s be the first integer with which rs=0. Then
    • rs−1=gcd(a;b), if s>0
    • b=gcd(a;b), if s=0

gcd(203;91)=rs−1=r1=7

UV form

Theorem

if d and c are greatest common divisors of numbers a and b, then d=c

Tldr

There can't be 2 different greatest common divisors

Theorem

If a and b - are natural numbers, then their greatest common divisor can be written in a form ua+vb, where u and v - integer numbers

Example

gcd(85;34) in form 85u+34v
gcd(85;34)=17=85∗1+34∗(−2)

Prime numbers

Definition
  • An integer greater than 1 is called prime if it has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself
  • ~ Otherwise, that number is called composite (can be written as a product of primes)
Theorem
  • Euclid's theorem
  • $ There are an infinite amount of prime numbers
Theorem
  • If natural number n is composite, then it has such prime divisor p, that p2<n
  • If p - prime number and p|ab, where a and b - natural numbers, then p|a or p|b
    • Generalized: If p divides a product of natural numbers, it at least divides one of those numbers
  • If prime number p divides a product of prime numbers, it is equal to at least one number from the product

Canonical form of number

n=p1α1∗p2α2∗⋯∗pkαk

  • p1,p2,p3,…,pk - different prime numbers
  • αi≥1,i=1,2,…,k
Example
  • 24=23∗31
  • 18=21∗32

Number of divisors

d(n)=(α1+1)∗(α2+1)∗⋯∗(αk+1)

Example
  • 24=23∗31, d(n)=(3+1)∗(1+1)=8
  • $ Check: (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24)

Coprime numbers

Definition
  • If greatest common divisor of numbers a and b is equal to 1, then they are called coprime.
  • Numbers agcd(a;b) and bgcd(a;b) are coprime
  • If a, b and c are integers, a and b are coprime and a|bc, then a|c
Example
  • gcd(17;3)=1
  • 3|(6*17), 3|6

Common multiple

Definition

Natural number m is a common multiple of numbers a and b, if a|m and b|m

Example

Multiples of 14 are 14,28,42,56,70,84,98,112,126,...
Multiples of 21 are 21,42,63,84,105,126,...

Least common multiple

Definition
  • Natural number m is called nearest common multiple of numbers a and b, if
    • It is common multiple
    • If a|n and b|n, then m|n (should divide all other common multiples)
  • $ Notion: lcm(a;b)
Theorem

If a and b - natural numbers, then gcd(a;b)∗lcm(a;b)=a∗b


Equations

Theorem

Say a, b and c are integers. Solution (x;y) of equation ax+by=c is pair of integers if, and only if c is divisible by gcd(a;b)
Then x0=ucgcd(a;b),   y0=vcgcd(a;b)Other solutions: x=x0=+btgcd(a;b),   y=y0−atgcd(a;b)Where
t - any integer
u and v are any solutions of equation gcd(a;b)=au+bv

Example

Solve the equation 85x+34y=51

  1. gcd(85;34)=17
  2. c=51 is divisible by 17
  3. 17=85∗1+34∗(−2)

Sieve of Eratosthenes

Let’s find prime numbers less than 101

  1. The first prime number is 2. Every second (except 2) is marked as not prime. They can be divided by 2
  2. The first unmarked number after 2 is 3. Every third number (except 3) is marked as not prime. They can be divided by 3
  3. The first unmarked number after 3 is 5. Every fifth number (except 5) is marked as not prime. They can be divided by 5
  4. The first unmarked number after 5 is 7. Every seventh number (except 7) is marked as not prime. They can be divided by 7
  5. $ We found all numbers. Why?
    • 100=10, the largest prime number smaller than 10 is 7
